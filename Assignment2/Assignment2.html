The Volcanoes of Major PNW Cities

By Owen Ferguson

Mt. Hood

From downtown Portland, Mt. Hood is roughly 40 miles to the east-southeast, and the drive usually takes about 1.5 to 2 hours depending on traffic and road conditions. The mountain is Oregon's tallest peak at approx. 11,249ft (3,429m) above sea level, and on clear days its summit stands out sharply above the Cascades and is easily viewable from Portland. A common "Indigenous" name given to Mt. Hood is Wy'east; however, this name has been disputed as not originally being an Indigenous name given to the mountain. Read more

Mt. Rainier

From downtown Seattle, Mt. Rainier sits about 60 miles to the southeast, with a driving time that is often around 2.5 to 3.5 hours to reach the park depending on your route and the season. Rainier is Washington's tallest mountain and one of the most prominent landmarks in the region, standing at 14,410ft (4,392m) above sea level, and can be seen on clear days from Seattle. It's Indigenous name is Tahoma, which is commonly used as a name for places and companies in the region (notably, the city of Tacoma). Read more

Mt. Baker

From downtown Vancouver, B.C., Mt. Baker is generally to the south-southeast about 143 miles away, and the drive to the mountain area is usually about 2.5 to 4 hours depending on border wait times and road conditions. Baker is a heavily glaciated volcano in the North Cascades, and its ice fields make it especially dramatic in satellite view. It stands at 10,781ft (3,286m) above sea level, and during clear days can be seen from as far as North Vancouver.
